Career June 2025

The Compounding Logic of Early Work

Every serious thing I have done in college — the research role, the internship, the platforms I built — was not required. Nobody assigned it. That is the point. The gap between students who do optional hard things and students who wait for structured opportunities is not a talent gap. It is a decision gap.

The compounding logic is straightforward: each piece of real work you complete makes the next slightly more accessible. You have a reference. You have demonstrated you can finish. You have something specific to talk about in an interview rather than something generic to describe. The credential, in early career, is the output — not the title.

I am not arguing for grinding. I am arguing for intention. The students who build real track records early are not necessarily working more hours. They are being more deliberate about which hours count. Finishing one real project that ships is worth more than three that stall. Writing one article that gets published is worth more than six drafts nobody reads. The discipline is completion, not volume.

Why this page exists

On writing.

Most student portfolios show what someone did. This page is an attempt to show how I think — which is more useful information for anyone considering working with me. A person who can do the analysis and explain it clearly is more valuable than someone who can only do one or the other.

These pieces are not optimized for reach or engagement. They are written to a specific standard: one clear argument, supported by specific evidence or experience, with no sentences that exist just to fill space. If a paragraph doesn't earn its place, it doesn't stay.

I will add pieces here as they meet that standard — not on a schedule, but when I have something worth saying. The goal is a small body of work that is genuinely good, not a large archive that is merely consistent.
