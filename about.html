How I think.

01

Data is a starting point, not an answer.

I have learned to be skeptical of outputs that look clean. The most useful analytical work starts with asking whether the right question is even being asked — and whether the data being used actually measures what people think it does. Good analysis is 30% computation and 70% epistemology.

02

Execution separates ideas from outcomes.

Most of what I have built — research platforms, policy articles, client-facing web tools — was not required of me by any course or job description. I work from a conviction that early-career credibility comes from finishing things, not pitching them. Completed projects compound; good intentions do not.

03

Communication is part of the analysis.

Writing policy articles for BreakingNewsABA taught me that a technically correct argument that cannot be read is not useful. I approach analytical communication the same way I approach code: every word should carry weight, every structure should serve the reader, and nothing should exist just to fill space.

04

Disciplines inform each other.

Studying Economics, Data Science, and Physics simultaneously is not accidental. Economics frames how people make decisions under constraints. Data Science gives tools to test those frames against evidence. Physics instills a comfort with abstraction and a demand for rigor. Each makes the others more useful.

Direction

Why data
& economics.

Analytics as a decision tool

I chose Economics because it is fundamentally about decision-making: how individuals, firms, and governments allocate scarce resources under competing pressures. Every framework in economics is a model of choice. Data Science gives those models empirical teeth. Together, they let me ask better questions and build more honest answers.

Real-world problems, not academic exercises

My research work on the IGODO project — studying how colonial language systems shaped African educational institutions — showed me that data analysis is most valuable when it is attached to a problem that matters. The ability to quantify historical impact, map policy outcomes, and translate findings for a public audience is a skill set with stakes. That is where I want to operate.

A career built on compounding signals

Each project I take on is a deliberate bet that doing serious work early creates more options later. The internship at Evident ABA, the research role on IGODO, the independent builds for family and academic clients — these are not resume line items. They are proof that I can enter a professional context and produce output that did not exist before I showed up.

Context

Beyond the classroom.

I co-run Fundisky Family, a luxury travel concierge business serving clients through an LLVC Diamond membership. Operating a real business as a student — managing client relationships, coordinating logistics, and building the company's web presence — has made me a more grounded and commercially aware thinker.

Independent Builder

I have built and deployed multiple multi-page websites from scratch — for academic research networks, family businesses, and institutional clients — using HTML, CSS, and JavaScript with no framework dependencies. Building things that go live and serve real users is a discipline that makes every other kind of work more concrete.

Community & Leadership

Recognized with a 2025 Lake Forest College Leadership Award, I take seriously the idea that influence is earned through reliability and service, not title. My involvement on campus reflects a belief that character is what converts competence into trust — and trust is what makes collaboration actually work.
